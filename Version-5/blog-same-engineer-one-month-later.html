The same engineer, one month later.

Scott Field, Chief Product & Technology Officer · July 2026

Give a strong engineer a rate manual, a coding agent, and enough time to understand the target platform, and they will probably produce something impressive.

Ask the same engineer to solve the same problem one month later and the result may be different. The model may have changed. The prompt will be different. The engineer will bring different context to the task. One solution may use a configuration-driven design; another may generate code. Both may be technically sound.

That variability is one of the reasons coding agents are useful — they explore possible solutions quickly and often find approaches a team would not have considered. But insurance technology has a requirement that plausibility does not satisfy: the product must stay consistent across platforms, teams, and years.

The question after the session

The fair question for a software company like Oversee: why wouldn’t a carrier build this itself with Claude Code?

A capable engineering team could use Claude Code to read product material and produce core-system configuration, specifications, or tests. They should experiment with it. The more useful question is what remains after the session ends.

There will be code. There may be documentation, a chat transcript, and a working implementation. What there will not be is the product — not the implementation in one target system, but the definition itself: the rates, rules, coverages, forms, classifications, payment plans, versions, and jurisdictional variations the carrier intended to implement.

If that definition does not exist independently, the next change begins by interpreting the source material again — a new endorsement arrives, a rate revision takes effect, the product enters another state, a distribution partner requires a variation, or the carrier acquires a business running another core. Someone points a model at the documents and asks for the next answer. The result may work — it may even be excellent — but it may not describe the product in quite the same way as the last answer.

Small differences become permanent differences

Insurance technology accumulates interpretations. A requirement is written in a document. One team translates it into a core platform, another into a portal, a third into a rating engine or servicing process. Over time, those interpretations drift.

Coding agents make each translation faster. They do not make the translations consistent. The same term may be represented differently by different teams. Effective dates may be handled differently. A rule may sit in configuration in one system and application code in another. A coverage variation may be explicit in one model and implied by a form selection somewhere else. None of these decisions looks wrong in isolation.

The risk appears when the carrier needs to change the product across all of them — and has to say which implementation reflects the intended product, which differences are deliberate, which are accidents of the engineer or prompt or model that generated them, and which version was in force when a policy was issued. Carriers answer those questions long after the original implementation team has moved on.

The first session rewards ingenuity

A good coding-agent session is compelling because it compresses a large amount of work into a short period of time. The first session rewards ingenuity. The hundredth product change rewards structure.

At scale, the carrier needs a consistent way to represent the product, validate changes, preserve versions, and generate artifacts for different target systems. The source of authority cannot be the most recent prompt, or whichever implementation happens to be easiest to inspect, or the engineer who remembers why a decision was made three years ago. It has to be the product definition itself.

Use the coding agent

Claude Code is a layer of software built on a model, and the model is built on years of other software, research, and infrastructure. Few engineering teams begin every project by rebuilding the layers beneath them. They choose where their own work matters and use mature tools for the rest.

Carriers should use coding agents. Their engineers should use them to understand systems, speed up implementation, generate tests, and work with the artifacts their tech stack requires. The question is what those agents are pointed at.

Point them at a folder of documents and every session rediscovers the structure of the product. Point them at existing system configuration and they reproduce the assumptions and inconsistencies already embedded there. Point them at a carrier-owned product definition and the models become interchangeable tools working from the same source of authority.

Software for the product

Oversee assumes coding models will keep improving. Individual technical capabilities inside any software platform can eventually be reproduced, and building a company on the claim that nobody else can generate similar code would be a weak position. The durable value is the asset left behind.

Oversee maintains a structured, versioned definition of the insurance product, independent of any one core platform or implementation partner. It generates the specifications, configuration, mappings, and tests required by each target system. The carrier owns the definition; the artifacts land in its repositories and move through its own review and release gates.

The next change can come from a different engineer, the next artifact from a different model, and the future execution from a different core platform — all of them beginning from the same definition. That is what insurance technology needs from this generation of AI: faster implementation without losing consistency or control.

The same engineer one month later may produce a different technical solution. They should not produce a different insurance product.
