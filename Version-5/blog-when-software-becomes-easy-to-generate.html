When software becomes easy to generate.

Scott Field, Chief Product & Technology Officer · July 2026

A capable engineer can now describe a problem to a coding agent and have a technically credible solution running the same day. The code may be good. The tests may pass. The result may be better than what a team would have produced manually a few years ago.

That capability is real, and any argument built on pretending otherwise will age badly.

But easier software generation does not remove the hardest constraint in insurance technology — keeping every implementation’s understanding of the product consistent. It makes that constraint more visible.

The obligation outlasts the implementation

An insurance product launch creates obligations that can last for decades. The carrier must continue to service the policies it issued: process endorsements and renewals, apply the correct rates and forms, support claims under the coverage sold, and explain which product version was in force at a particular point in time.

The team that launched it moves on, and so, eventually, do the engineers, the models, and the core platform itself. The product does not get that option. The policies written against it remain in force, renewing and producing claims for years.

This is why speed in insurance technology has to be understood carefully. Producing the first implementation quickly is valuable. But the first implementation is a small share of the product’s lifetime cost, and the obligation it creates is long.

More software does not create more control

For most of the history of enterprise technology, software was expensive to produce. That cost limited how many local solutions an organization could create. Coding agents change those economics.

A product team creates one implementation; a platform team creates another; an acquired business keeps its own; a new distribution channel generates something tailored to its immediate needs. Each solution may be reasonable on its own. Together, they may describe the same insurance product in incompatible ways.

One team represents a coverage as configuration. Another embeds it in code. A third reconstructs it from documents whenever a change is required. Each makes different assumptions about versions, effective dates, jurisdictional variation, and precedence. The software works. The carrier’s understanding of the product becomes less coherent.

Insurance companies already live with this. Product knowledge sits across documents, spreadsheets, system configuration, shared drives, and individual expertise, and every platform team interprets the same requirements in the language of its own tech stack. Making each interpretation faster does not remove the repetition. It speeds it up.

The constraint is coherence

The old constraint was the cost of writing software. The next one is the cost of keeping every implementation consistent.

Insurance technology is rarely built around one system, one team, or one moment in time. A carrier may need the same product to operate across several core platforms, a distribution stack, acquired systems, servicing tools, and downstream financial processes — and to support multiple generations of the product at once.

A coding agent can generate a plausible solution for any one of those environments. Every environment still depends on the same facts: which rates apply, which rules govern eligibility, which forms belong to this version, when the change became effective, where the requirement came from, and which implementations must change with it. Those facts cannot be recovered reliably by generating another implementation. They require something stable that exists independently of every implementation.

What should survive

The code may change, and in many cases it should. The carrier may replace a platform, regenerate configuration, adopt a better model, or redesign an integration. There is no reason to treat every technical artifact as permanent.

But the meaning of the product must survive those changes. The carrier needs a durable definition of its rates, rules, coverages, forms, classifications, payment plans, versions, and variations — carrier-owned, reviewable, and independent of any one core platform or implementation partner. From that definition, software and configuration can be generated again and again as the tech stack changes.

Without it, every new generation begins by reconstructing the product from documents, existing code, system behavior, and institutional memory. That is not a new problem. It is the problem insurance technology has carried for decades, now operating at the speed of modern models.

Better models make the definition more important

Coding agents will continue to improve. They will understand larger codebases, complete more of the implementation work that currently requires large engineering teams, and do it for less. That is good for insurance technology.

But the more capable the models become, the more important it is to give them something stable to work from. A model should not have to infer the insurance product differently every time it generates an artifact. It should work from a structured definition that the carrier owns and governs. That is how faster generation becomes an asset the carrier keeps, rather than another source of product sprawl.

Oversee builds software for the life of an insurance product. The platform maintains the carrier-owned product definition and generates what each core system needs. The systems can change and the models can improve; the product remains understandable, consistent, and under the carrier’s control.

Software is becoming easier to produce. The insurance product still has to last.
